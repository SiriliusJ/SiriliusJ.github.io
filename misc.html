PC Builds

Outside research, a small but important part of my skill set is building and maintaining high-performance PCs and lab workstations. Over the years I have built more than 30 desktops and repaired over 50 laptops and lab machines, taking care of OS installation, environment setup, remote access configuration, troubleshooting, and long-term maintenance. For example, I helped a WashU EECE lab revive aging workstations by working around obsolete logins and recovering experiment data and legacy engineering software. It started as a hobby but has become a practical infrastructure skill that I hope will directly support the lab I join for my PhD.

Earliest PC Build

2022 · Undergraduate daily driver · Gaming and everyday work

This was my late-undergraduate main PC, built in 2022 and used until I left for the U.S. It started as a balanced machine for gaming and coursework and gradually became my testbed for OS installs, remote access, virtual machines, local NAS and capture-card setups, and long-running image generation experiments, where I standardized the workflow I now use when building and maintaining other systems.

Most Expensive Build

2023 · Personal flagship build · Self-funded reward

This is the most expensive desktop I have ever owned, a personal flagship that I fully paid for with income from side projects as a reward to myself. I used it as the main machine for my undergraduate thesis and for all of my generative modeling experiments. It runs a dual Windows and Linux setup, supports Wake on LAN and remote control, and has been my platform for trying out high performance GPU workflows such as NVLink; I still use this PC today.

US Budget/Scavenged Build

2024 · St. Louis · Built almost entirely from discarded parts

After moving to the U.S., I assembled this PC almost entirely from spare parts that people left behind when they moved or upgraded. It is now my main Windows workstation on campus. I tested and cleaned each used component, updated BIOS and drivers, and checked stability so that the final machine could handle daily work, remote meetings and light experiments, with extra parts left over as backups and spares.

Parking Lot Emoji Bollard

2021 · Shopping mall parking lot near UCAS · Xi’an

Story▾

I spotted this painted concrete bollard near the entrance of a shopping mall parking lot by my undergraduate campus. It was part of a new series of emoji-style bollards, but people told me that right after they were installed, there was a car accident.

This one got hit, rolled all the way to this spot in the middle of the lane, and somehow ended up standing upright again. The completely unbothered expression plus that story makes it feel even cooler to me.
